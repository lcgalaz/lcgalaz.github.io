The Modern Lender of Last Resort

This section summarizes the key findings of my undergraduate thesis, completed in 2024 as part of the requirements for my Bachelor's degree in Economics. The research explores the historical evolution of the lender of last resort (LOLR) function in central banking—from its theoretical origins in 19th-century Britain to its transformation during the 2008 Global Financial Crisis (GFC).

What follows is a summary of the main findings, organized to highlight how the LOLR function has evolved across time, institutions, and crisis contexts.

London’s Lombard Street – historic birthplace of central banking thought

Approach and Main Contributions

Using a historical-institutional approach, the study analyzes how central banks have adapted their LOLR role in response to systemic financial instability, shaped by legal mandates, regulatory changes, and crisis-specific demands.

The analysis draws on primary sources from central bank archives, relevant literature in monetary and financial history, and contributions by early monetary theorists such as Sir Francis Baring, Henry Thornton, and Walter Bagehot.

The central contribution of the thesis lies in identifying the emergence of a “modern LOLR” framework—one that reflects a significant departure from classical formulations.

This modern version is characterized by broader eligibility criteria for counterparties, more flexible instruments, the creation of new liquidity facilities, active market interventions, and increasing cross-border coordination. These features have become critical components of central banking in the post-GFC era.
