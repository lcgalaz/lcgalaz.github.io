Undergraduate Thesis

First independent research project, conducted under the supervision of Professor Carlo Panico.

The Modern Lender of Last Resort

This research traces the historical evolution of central banks’ role as lender of last resort (LOLR), from the 19th-century practices of the Bank of England to the multifaceted responses of the Federal Reserve, the ECB, and the BoE during the 2008 Global Financial Crisis.

Through a historical-institutional lens, the project explores how this function emerged, adapted, and expanded in the face of increasingly complex financial crises. It highlights the transition from rigid frameworks to more flexible, discretionary tools, and how legal mandates and political economy constraints have shaped intervention capacity.

  • Analyzes the intellectual foundations from Baring, Thornton, and Bagehot.
  • Compares institutional responses across the Great Depression and the 2008 GFC.
  • Explores modern LOLR features: wide collateral acceptance, international swap lines, and market-maker-of-last-resort mechanisms.

The study concludes that there is no single recipe for LOLR interventions; rather, central banks adapt their tools to the nature of each crisis. The function has grown more sophisticated, but also more politically charged, raising important questions for institutional design, accountability, and global coordination.
